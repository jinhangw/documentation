Best update rate and resolution

The tradeoffs to higher update rate and resolution are increased storage, data transfer and computation. So far (beginning of 2013) our system has been taxing on these three but they have not yet reach a point where they become roadblocks. We therefore want to find the point where an increase in update rate and/or resolution will only give marginal improvements to the data quality.

Resolution

Because we are taking data from a moving platform, the images we are taking have motion blurr. There will be little improvements in the data quality if the pixel resolution is smaller than the motion blurr. Therefore we will calculate the point where the pixel resolution is the same as the motion blurr. 

At a typical urban speed of 35 mph (15.6 m/s) and the fastest shutter speed of the camera (1/2000 s, Galaxy Camera) the motion blurr is 7.8 mm in the driving direction. We consider the scene at a distance of 5m from the camera and the camera is at a height of 1.2m. The 7.8 mm is projected onto the vertical and becomes the size of 7.8mm*1.2m/5m = 1.9 mm.

        Result: Vertical motion blurr is about 2 mm at 5 m distance.

The horizontal and vertical resolutions are the same. Horizontally we want to cover about one lane (3 m) at a distance of 5m. If each pixel covers 2 mm, we would need 1500 pixels. The horizontal resolution of 1080p video is about 2000, i.e. the resolution of 1080p video is sufficient

Result: If the camera is mounted at 1.2 m and set up to take 1080p video that covers one lane (3 m) at a distance of 5 m, then the pixel resolution is about the same as the motion blurr resulting from a speed of 35 mph.

Update rate

To get sufficient overlap between consecutive images we want the vehicle to move only about 1 m between frames. At 35 mph (15.6 m/s) and a 10 Hz update rate the movement between frames is 1.56 m. 


Compression

We compared the compression rate between video (standard HD from camera) with JPG. The compression is about the same, i.e. the size of a video (48 Mb) is only a bit less than the size of the individual frames saved as JPG files (about 55 Mb, but varies considerably: 50Mb - 100 Mb). Therefore file size is not an improtant point to consider if deciding between saving the data as HD vidoe or as JPG files.
